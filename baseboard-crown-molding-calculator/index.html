    Why This Matters

    Buying too little molding is a bigger problem than it sounds. Molding is sold in fixed lengths (usually 8, 12, or 16 feet), and if you run short mid-project, your new batch may not match the sheen or profile of the original — especially with painted MDF. You'd have to repaint the entire room.

    On the flip side, over-buying by 30% just adds waste and cost. A 12×14 room needs roughly 48 linear feet of perimeter. With a standard 10% waste factor and one doorway, you're looking at about 45 usable linear feet — meaning 4–5 eight-foot boards. That's a manageable Saturday project for most homeowners. Crown molding on the same room takes longer (3–5 hours) because the cuts are more complex.

    This tool is built for real weekend projects — whether you're finishing a basement, upgrading a living room, or trimming out a new build. Contractors use the same perimeter math, just faster. Now you can too.

    How It's Calculated

    The core formula is simple:

    • Perimeter = (Length + Width) × 2
    • Net footage = Perimeter − (Number of doors × door width)
    • With waste = Net footage × (1 + waste%)
    • Boards needed = ceil(Footage with waste ÷ board length)

    For crown molding, the same perimeter is used — crown runs at ceiling height on all four walls. The board count uses standard 8-foot lengths. Cost is calculated as: Total footage × price per linear foot.

    Tips & Common Mistakes

    • Don't skip the waste factor. Miter cuts waste 2–3 inches per joint, and every corner has two. A 12×14 room has 8 corners — that's potentially 24 inches of waste just from corners.
    • Measure twice, cut once. Round up your measurements rather than rounding down. An extra foot of molding is worth far less than a second trip to the hardware store.
    • Match profiles before you buy. Bring a sample piece of existing molding to the store. Phone photos often miss subtle profile differences.
    • Crown molding requires a compound miter saw. If you're doing inside corners with coped joints (the pro method), factor in extra time and material for practice cuts.
    • Primed vs. unprimed: Buy primed MDF molding if you plan to paint — it saves significant prep time and holds paint better than bare wood.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    How much does baseboard molding cost on average?

    Basic painted MDF baseboard runs $0.75–$1.50 per linear foot. Mid-grade finger-jointed pine is $1.50–$2.50, and solid oak or poplar for staining runs $3–$6+ per foot. For a typical 12×14 bedroom, expect $50–$150 total material cost depending on style.

    What's the difference between baseboard and crown molding?

    Baseboard molding runs along the bottom of walls where they meet the floor — it hides the gap and protects drywall from kicks and scuffs. Crown molding runs along the top of walls at the ceiling and is purely decorative, adding visual height and elegance. Crown is harder to install because it requires compound miter cuts.

    Should I subtract window openings like door openings?

    For baseboard, no — baseboard doesn't run under windows (it stops at the window casing). For crown molding, windows are typically framed with returns, so the crown still runs across the top. This calculator accounts for doors only, which is the most common deduction. Add window adjustments manually if needed.

    How long does it take to install molding?

    A beginner with basic tools can do a standard 12×14 room of baseboard in 3–5 hours including nailing, filling nail holes, and caulking. Crown molding in the same room takes 5–8 hours for a beginner. Experienced installers can do both in 2–3 hours total.
