    How to Use This Drywall Calculator

    Enter your room's length, width, and wall height. Choose whether you're drywalling walls, the ceiling, or both. Add any doors or windows to subtract their area. Select your sheet size, thickness, and adjust the waste factor for your skill level. Hit Calculate Sheets and you'll get an exact sheet count, cost estimate, and shopping list.

    Why This Matters

    Buying too few sheets means an extra trip to the hardware store mid-project — often on a Saturday when the store is packed and you've already got mud drying on one wall. Buying too many wastes money. A standard 12×10-foot bedroom with 8-foot ceilings needs about 22–24 sheets for walls, yet most homeowners either eyeball it or use a rough rule-of-thumb that doesn't account for their specific layout.

    The waste factor matters more than people realize. A first-timer working around windows, outlets, and corners will waste 20–25% of material on cuts. An experienced drywaller may only waste 8–10%. Choosing the right sheet size also saves money — 4×12 sheets mean fewer seams and faster installation in rooms with 9-foot ceilings, but they're heavier and harder for one person to hang.

    Contractors estimate roughly 1 sheet per 32 square feet for basic rooms, but that figure can be off by 30% or more in complex spaces. This calculator does the math properly.

    How It's Calculated

    The formula is straightforward:

    • Wall area = 2 × (length + width) × height
    • Ceiling area = length × width
    • Net area = total area − openings (doors, windows)
    • Gross area = net area × (1 + waste factor)
    • Sheets needed = ⌈ gross area ÷ sheet size ⌉ (always rounds up)

    We always round up to the nearest whole sheet — you can't buy a fraction of a sheet. The waste factor compensates for cuts around corners, outlets, angled ceilings, and general material loss during installation.

    Tips & Common Mistakes

    • Always round up, never down. Being one sheet short is far more costly than having one extra leaning in the garage.
    • Use 5/8" fire-rated drywall in garages and on shared walls between living spaces and garages — it's required by most building codes.
    • Don't forget the ceiling. Many beginners calculate only walls and forget the ceiling adds another 15–25% of material.
    • Stack sheets flat when storing — leaning them against a wall causes warping within days, making installation much harder.
    • Order 10% extra for small repairs while you're at it — patches from plumbing and electrical work show up months later and you'll want matching material.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    How many drywall sheets do I need for a 12×12 room?
    A standard 12×12 room with 8-foot ceilings needs approximately 19–21 sheets for walls only (including a 10% waste factor). Adding the ceiling brings it to around 24–27 sheets. Subtract a sheet or two if you have large windows, and always buy at least one extra.
    What's the difference between 4×8 and 4×12 drywall sheets?
    4×12 sheets cover 50% more area per sheet and create fewer seams (seams are where cracks appear and where finishing takes longest). However, each 4×12 sheet of 1/2" drywall weighs about 95 lbs vs. 57 lbs for 4×8, making them difficult to hang alone. Use 4×12 for 9-foot or 10-foot ceilings where they fit without cutting.
    Should I use 1/2" or 5/8" drywall?
    1/2" is standard for most interior walls and ceilings in residential construction. Use 5/8" (Type X) for garages, basement ceilings under living areas, and walls adjacent to mechanical rooms — it provides a 1-hour fire rating. Some jurisdictions require 5/8" on ceilings for spans over 16 inches on center.
    How do I calculate drywall for a room with a vaulted ceiling?
    For vaulted ceilings, measure the actual sloped ceiling length (not the floor-to-peak height) and multiply by the room width. You can use a tape measure along the ceiling slope, or calculate it as: sloped length = √(half-width² + rise²). Add 15–20% extra waste for vaulted ceilings due to the angled cuts required.
