How to Use This Body Fat Percentage Calculator

Select your preferred unit system (Imperial or Metric) and estimation method (Navy or BMI). Enter your measurements — height, and circumferences for the Navy method, or height and weight for the BMI method. Hit Calculate and instantly see your estimated body fat percentage, health category, and fat/lean mass breakdown.

For the most accurate results, use the US Navy Method with a soft measuring tape. Measure your neck at its narrowest point, waist at the navel, and hips at the widest point (females only).

Why This Matters

Body weight alone doesn't tell the full story. Two people can weigh exactly 180 lbs — one might be 12% body fat with 20 lbs of fat and 160 lbs of lean muscle, while another is 28% body fat with 50 lbs of fat. Same scale number, completely different health picture.

According to the American Council on Exercise (ACE), essential fat for men is 2–5%, while women need 10–13% for basic physiological function. Athletes typically carry 6–13% (men) or 14–20% (women). Above 25% for men and 32% for women is classified as obese.

Tracking body fat percentage is especially valuable when you're strength training — you might gain weight on the scale while actually improving your body composition by building muscle and losing fat. It's also a far better predictor of metabolic health, cardiovascular risk, and insulin sensitivity than BMI alone. Whether you're preparing for a fitness goal, monitoring health during aging, or just curious — knowing your body fat percentage gives you actionable data that body weight cannot.

How It's Calculated

US Navy Method (Hodgdon & Beckett, 1984): Uses height and circumference measurements. This is the method used by the US military for fitness assessments.

Men: BF% = 86.010 × log₁₀(waist − neck) − 70.041 × log₁₀(height) + 36.76

Women: BF% = 163.205 × log₁₀(waist + hip − neck) − 97.684 × log₁₀(height) − 78.387

All measurements in inches. The result is a percentage that estimates adipose tissue as a share of total body mass.

BMI Method (Deurenberg et al., 1991): Uses BMI, age, and sex. Less precise but useful when circumference measurements aren't available.

BF% = (1.20 × BMI) + (0.23 × age) − (10.8 × sex) − 5.4
where sex = 1 for male, 0 for female; BMI = weight(kg) / height(m)²

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Measure in the morning: Waist circumference can fluctuate by 1–3 cm throughout the day due to food and water intake. Take measurements first thing after waking for consistency.
  • Exhale before measuring waist: Measure at the end of a normal exhale — don't suck in or puff out. The tape should be snug but not compressing the skin.
  • Hip measurement is at the widest point: For females using the Navy method, measure the hip at the fullest point, not the hip bone (iliac crest).
  • Navy method ≠ DEXA scan: The Navy formula has a margin of error of ±3–4% compared to DEXA (the gold standard). Use it to track trends over time, not as an absolute number.
  • BMI method is least accurate: It doesn't account for muscle mass distribution. A muscular person will appear to have higher body fat than they actually do using this method.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is the US Navy Method compared to a DEXA scan?

The US Navy Method typically has an error margin of ±3–4 percentage points compared to DEXA (Dual-Energy X-ray Absorptiometry), which is considered the gold standard. It tends to overestimate body fat in very lean individuals and underestimate it in people who carry more visceral fat internally. That said, it's one of the most accessible and validated field methods available and is widely used for tracking trends over time.

What's a healthy body fat percentage for my age?

According to ACE guidelines, "fitness" ranges are 14–17% for men and 21–24% for women. However, acceptable ranges shift slightly with age — it's normal to carry slightly more fat as you age. For men over 50, up to 22% is generally considered acceptable. For women over 50, up to 30% is within a healthy range. The most important factor is staying out of the "obese" category and maintaining lean muscle mass.

Can I use this calculator if I'm very muscular or an athlete?

The Navy Method works reasonably well for athletic individuals, as it's circumference-based rather than weight-based. However, if you're extremely muscular (like a competitive bodybuilder), the formula may slightly overestimate your body fat. The BMI method is particularly poor for athletes. For the most accurate measurement, consider hydrostatic weighing or a DEXA scan.

Why does the women's Navy formula include a hip measurement?

Women naturally carry more subcutaneous fat in the hip and gluteal region (gynoid fat distribution) compared to men, who tend to accumulate fat around the abdominal area (android distribution). Including the hip measurement in the female formula captures this sex-specific fat distribution pattern, making the estimate significantly more accurate for women than if hips were excluded.
