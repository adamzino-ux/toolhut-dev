How to Use This Heart Rate Zone Calculator

Enter your age and resting heart rate (measured first thing in the morning), then click Calculate My Zones. You'll instantly see your five training zones in beats per minute. You can also override the max heart rate if you've had it measured via a stress test or VO₂ max test. Switch between the Karvonen method (which accounts for your resting heart rate) and the simpler % Max HR method using the tabs.

Why Heart Rate Zones Matter

Training without knowing your zones is like driving without a speedometer — you have no idea if you're going easy enough to recover or hard enough to improve. Heart rate zones let you target specific physiological adaptations with each workout.

A 35-year-old runner with a resting HR of 58 bpm, for example, has a Zone 2 range (aerobic base) of roughly 124–140 bpm using the Karvonen method. Staying in this range for 80% of their runs builds mitochondrial density and fat-burning efficiency without overloading the body. Meanwhile, Zone 4 (tempo, ~157–167 bpm) improves lactate threshold — the pace you can sustain for a hard 10K.

Elite coaches use the 80/20 principle: 80% of training at low intensity (Zones 1–2), 20% at high intensity (Zones 4–5). Without a calculator like this one, most recreational athletes accidentally train too hard on easy days and too easy on hard days — the "gray zone" that leads to burnout and plateaus.

Heart rate zones are also used in cardiac rehabilitation, weight loss programs, and triathlon periodization. Knowing your zones is fundamental whether you're a beginner walker or an Ironman finisher.

How It's Calculated

Max Heart Rate: Estimated as 220 − age (you can override this with a measured value).

Karvonen Method (Heart Rate Reserve):

Heart Rate Reserve (HRR) = Max HR − Resting HR
Target HR = (HRR × % intensity) + Resting HR

For example, Zone 3 lower bound at 60% for a person with Max HR 185 and Resting HR 60:
(185 − 60) × 0.60 + 60 = 75 + 60 = 135 bpm

% Max HR Method: Simply multiplies your max HR by each zone's percentage range — e.g. Zone 2 = 60–70% of 185 = 111–130 bpm. The Karvonen method is generally considered more accurate as it personalizes for your fitness level (reflected by resting HR).

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Measure resting HR accurately. Don't use yesterday's value — measure it after at least 5 quiet minutes lying down, ideally right after waking.
  • The 220 − age formula is an estimate. Up to 10–12 bpm variation from actual max HR is common. A supervised max test or recent race effort gives a much better number.
  • Use a chest strap for training. Wrist-based optical sensors lag by 10–20 seconds and are less accurate during intervals — fine for steady-state, unreliable for Zone 4–5 work.
  • Don't ignore Zone 2. Most beginners spend too much time in Zone 3 (the "junk zone") — hard enough to be tiring, but not hard enough to drive adaptation.
  • Zones shift over time. As your fitness improves, your resting HR drops, and your zones change. Recalculate every 8–12 weeks.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a normal resting heart rate?

For adults, a normal resting heart rate is 60–100 bpm. Well-trained athletes often sit between 40–60 bpm. If your resting HR is consistently above 100, speak with a doctor. The lower your resting HR (within normal range), the higher your heart rate reserve and the wider your training zones.

Which method is more accurate — Karvonen or % Max HR?

The Karvonen method is widely considered more accurate because it factors in your resting heart rate, which reflects your cardiovascular fitness level. A highly trained athlete with a resting HR of 45 will have different effective zones than a sedentary person with a resting HR of 80, even at the same age and max HR. The % Max HR method ignores this and tends to underestimate upper zones for fit individuals.

Can I use this calculator for cycling, swimming, or rowing?

Yes, though keep in mind that max heart rate can differ by sport. Swimming HR tends to run 10–13 bpm lower than running due to horizontal position and water cooling. Cycling HR sits about 5–8 bpm below running for most people. If you train seriously in multiple sports, consider measuring your max HR sport-specifically for best results.

How do I find my actual maximum heart rate?

The most practical field test is a 3-minute all-out effort — warm up well, then do a hard 1-mile run or 3×3-minute intervals escalating to maximum effort. Your peak HR near the end is close to your true max. For clinical accuracy, a treadmill stress test administered by a physician is the gold standard, particularly important if you're over 40 or have cardiovascular risk factors.
