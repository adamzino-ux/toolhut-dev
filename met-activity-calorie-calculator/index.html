How to Use This Calories Burned by Activity Calculator

Enter your body weight (in kg or lbs), age, and desired workout duration using the slider. Then pick an activity category and select your specific exercise. Hit Calculate and you'll instantly see total calories burned, the per-minute and per-hour burn rate, the MET value of your activity, and a color-coded intensity zone.

Why This Matters

Most fitness apps give vague calorie estimates — this tool uses validated MET (Metabolic Equivalent of Task) values from the Compendium of Physical Activities to give you science-backed numbers. Here's why that matters in practice:

Say you're a 75 kg person trying to lose half a pound per week (~250 kcal/day deficit). A 30-minute jog at moderate pace (MET ~7) burns roughly 263 kcal. That single run effectively covers your entire daily deficit. Contrast that with 30 minutes of gentle yoga (MET ~2.5) which burns only ~94 kcal — a meaningful difference in planning.

Athletes use this to plan training load. Rehab patients use it to track safe activity levels. Weight managers use it to balance food intake. Even office workers benefit — knowing that 45 minutes of brisk walking burns ~300 kcal can motivate a lunchtime stroll instead of a desk lunch.

The per-hour burn rate is especially useful when comparing activities of different durations, while the intensity zone helps you understand cardiovascular demand beyond just calories.

How It's Calculated

This calculator uses the standard MET formula from exercise science:

Calories (kcal) = MET × Body Weight (kg) × Duration (hours)

MET (Metabolic Equivalent of Task) represents how many times more energy an activity requires compared to sitting at rest (1 MET ≈ 1 kcal/kg/hour). A MET of 7 means the activity burns 7× the energy of rest. Walking leisurely is ~2.5 MET; cycling vigorously is ~10 MET; competitive running can reach 14+ MET.

The formula naturally accounts for body weight because heavier individuals require more energy to move, which is physiologically accurate. Age is displayed for reference; advanced models apply small corrections but the standard formula remains industry-standard for general use.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Weight matters more than you think. A 90 kg person burns ~28% more calories than a 70 kg person doing the same activity for the same duration.
  • Intensity is key. "Cycling" spans MET 4–14+ depending on pace. Always pick the sub-activity that matches your actual effort level.
  • Don't double-count. These are gross calories (including resting metabolic rate during exercise), so don't add them on top of a full TDEE calculation — you'd overestimate burn.
  • Duration accuracy counts. People commonly overestimate workout duration by 15–20%. Track actual active time, not locker-room-to-locker-room time.
  • MET values are averages. Fitness level, terrain, heat, and equipment all affect real-world burn. Use this as a reliable estimate, not a precise measurement.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a MET value and why does it matter?

MET stands for Metabolic Equivalent of Task. It's a standard unit measuring the energy cost of an activity relative to rest (1 MET). A MET of 6 means you're burning 6× more energy than sitting still. MET values allow researchers and practitioners to compare activities across different body weights and fitness levels using a single universal number.

How accurate is this calorie calculator?

The MET formula is the most widely validated method for estimating exercise energy expenditure without lab equipment. Studies show it's accurate within 10–20% for most activities in average-weight adults. Accuracy decreases for very high-intensity interval training, swimming (water resistance varies), and activities with significant skill components that affect actual effort.

Does body weight really affect calorie burn that much?

Yes — directly and linearly. If you weigh 100 kg instead of 70 kg, you burn 43% more calories doing the exact same activity. This is because moving a heavier body requires proportionally more muscular force and cardiovascular output. It's one reason why initial weight loss sometimes temporarily increases caloric burn from exercise before diminishing as weight decreases.

Can I use this for weight loss planning?

Absolutely. One pound of fat ≈ 3,500 kcal. To lose 1 lb/week, you need a 500 kcal/day deficit through diet and/or exercise. Use this tool to identify activities that fit your schedule and fitness level to contribute to that deficit. Combine with a dietary calorie tracker for the most effective results, and aim for a balanced approach with both cardio and strength training.
