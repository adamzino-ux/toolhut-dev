How to Use This TDEE Calculator

Enter your age, sex, weight, and height, then select your typical weekly activity level. Choose a goal — from aggressive fat loss to aggressive bulking — and adjust the macro sliders to your preferred split. Hit "Calculate" to instantly see your TDEE, goal calories, and exact daily macro targets in grams.

Why This Matters

Your TDEE is the single most important number in nutrition. It tells you exactly how many calories your body burns in a day, accounting for everything from your resting metabolism to how much you move. Without knowing your TDEE, you're essentially guessing — and most people guess wrong by 300–600 calories per day.

Consider a 35-year-old moderately active man at 185 lbs: his TDEE might be around 2,750 calories. If he wants to lose 1 lb per week, he needs to eat around 2,250 calories — not the generic "2,000 calorie diet" that doesn't fit his body. Undereating by too much can cause muscle loss and metabolic adaptation; overeating even by 100 calories a day leads to 10 lbs of fat gain per year.

The macro breakdown matters because 2,500 calories of pizza affects your body very differently than 2,500 calories built around lean protein, complex carbs, and healthy fats. High protein intake (at least 0.7–1g per lb of bodyweight) is especially critical for preserving muscle while in a deficit and for building new muscle while in a surplus.

How It's Calculated

This calculator uses the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, considered the most accurate BMR formula for most adults:

  • Men: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) + 5
  • Women: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) − 161

TDEE = BMR × Activity Multiplier (1.2 for sedentary → 1.9 for extra active). Goal calories are then set by adding or subtracting a caloric surplus/deficit corresponding to ±0.5 or ±1 lb of fat per week (1 lb fat ≈ 3,500 calories, so ±500 cal/day = ±1 lb/week). Macros are divided from goal calories based on your chosen percentage split, then converted: protein & carbs = 4 cal/g; fat = 9 cal/g.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Don't underestimate activity level. "Moderately active" means you're intentionally exercising 3–5 days a week — desk workers who take occasional walks are usually "lightly active."
  • Prioritize protein. When cutting, keep protein at 0.8–1.2g per lb of bodyweight to preserve muscle. Protein also has the highest thermic effect of food (~25–30% of calories burned during digestion).
  • Recalculate every 10–15 lbs. As your weight changes, so does your TDEE. The number from 6 months ago may be 100–200 calories off today.
  • Track for at least 2 weeks before adjusting. Weight fluctuates daily due to water and glycogen. Real fat loss/gain trends only become visible over 1–2 weeks.
  • Aggressive deficits backfire. Eating more than 500 calories below TDEE typically leads to muscle loss, fatigue, and rebound overeating — a moderate 250–500 calorie deficit is more sustainable.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is a TDEE calculator?

The Mifflin-St Jeor equation is accurate within ±10% for most people. The biggest source of error is activity level estimation — most people overestimate how active they are. Treat your TDEE as a starting point, track your weight for 2 weeks, and adjust by 100–200 calories if the scale isn't moving as expected.

What's the difference between BMR and TDEE?

BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) is how many calories your body burns at complete rest — just to keep your organs functioning. TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) multiplies BMR by an activity factor to account for the calories you burn moving, exercising, and going about daily life. TDEE is the number you actually need for planning your diet.

Should I eat at TDEE on rest days and less on workout days?

This approach (calorie cycling) works for some people but is unnecessary for most. Your metabolism doesn't reset daily — weekly totals matter more than daily precision. Keeping calories consistent is simpler and equally effective. If you prefer cycling, you can eat 100–200 more on training days and 100–200 less on rest days.

Why is my calculated TDEE higher than I expected?

Many people underestimate their calorie needs because they compare their TDEE to generic diet advice. Standard 1,200 or 1,500 calorie recommendations are designed for small, sedentary women and are inappropriate for most active adults. If your TDEE seems high, trust the math — eating significantly below it for extended periods can slow your metabolism.
