How to Use This TDEE Calculator

Enter your age, sex, height, weight, and activity level. Optionally add your body fat percentage for a more precise estimate using the Katch-McArdle formula. Select your goal — fat loss, maintenance, or muscle gain — then adjust the macro sliders to fit your diet preference. Hit Calculate to instantly see your BMR, TDEE, calorie target, and gram-level macros.

Why This Matters

Most people fail their diet because they're eating the wrong number of calories — not because of willpower. Your TDEE is the single most important number in any nutrition plan. For example, a 175 lb, moderately active 30-year-old male has a TDEE around 2,700 calories. Eating 500 below that (~2,200) creates a 1 lb/week deficit. Eating 300 above it (~3,000) supports steady muscle gain without excessive fat.

This matters whether you're a beginner trying to lose 20 lbs, an athlete in an off-season bulk, or someone just trying to maintain weight through a stressful period. Knowing your macros — not just calories — tells you how to structure meals. 150g of protein for a 175 lb person means including protein in every meal. 40% carbs from 2,700 calories means about 270g of carbs, which guides rice, pasta, and fruit portions concretely.

How It's Calculated

We use two BMR formulas depending on whether you provide body fat:

Mifflin-St Jeor (no body fat):
Male: BMR = (10 × kg) + (6.25 × cm) − (5 × age) + 5
Female: BMR = (10 × kg) + (6.25 × cm) − (5 × age) − 161
Katch-McArdle (with body fat):
Lean Mass = weight × (1 − body fat %)
BMR = 370 + (21.6 × lean mass in kg)

TDEE = BMR × Activity Multiplier. Your calorie target is then adjusted by goal: −500 cal for fat loss (≈1 lb/week), +300 cal for muscle gain, or unchanged for maintenance. Macros are split from your target calories: protein and carbs = 4 cal/g, fat = 9 cal/g.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Don't overestimate activity. Most people who "go to the gym 3x/week" are sedentary the rest of the day. "Moderate" (1.55×) is the right pick for most gym-goers.
  • Protein first, adjust others around it. Aim for 0.7–1g per lb of bodyweight. Use the slider to match. High protein protects muscle during fat loss.
  • Recalculate every 10–15 lbs. As your weight changes, your TDEE changes. Stalls happen when people don't update their numbers.
  • Use body fat % if you can. Katch-McArdle is more accurate for lean athletes and overweight individuals where the standard formula can over/underestimate BMR.
  • Fat loss: don't go below 1,200 cal (women) or 1,500 cal (men). Severe deficits spike hunger hormones and cause muscle loss, not just fat loss.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between BMR and TDEE?

BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) is the calories your body burns at complete rest — just to keep organs functioning. TDEE multiplies BMR by your activity level, giving a real-world estimate of calories burned throughout the day including exercise, movement, and digestion.

How accurate is this calculator?

The Mifflin-St Jeor formula predicts BMR within ~10% for most people. Providing your body fat percentage with the Katch-McArdle method is generally more accurate for athletes and those with higher or lower body fat. Real-world results depend on food logging accuracy and metabolic individuality.

How should I split my macros?

It depends on your goal and preferences. A good starting point: 30% protein, 40% carbs, 30% fat for general fitness. For fat loss, try higher protein (35–40%) to preserve muscle. For endurance athletes, carbs up to 50–55% support training. Low-carb dieters might push fat to 50–60%.

Should I eat my TDEE or my target calories?

Eat your target calories — which is your TDEE adjusted for your goal. If you want to lose fat, your target is TDEE minus a deficit (e.g., 500 cal). Eating at TDEE means maintenance. The calculator shows your adjusted target prominently as the main number.
