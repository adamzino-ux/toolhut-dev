Why This Matters

Knowing your ideal weight range is a useful starting point for health planning — but it's more nuanced than a single number. A 5'10" (178 cm) male, for example, has an ideal weight between roughly 155–175 lbs (70–79 kg) depending on the formula used. These ranges reflect what population studies have found to correlate with lower risks of cardiovascular disease, metabolic disorders, and all-cause mortality.

Athletes, bodybuilders, and pregnant women should treat these numbers as guidelines rather than targets — someone with high muscle mass may be "overweight" by formula but perfectly healthy. Similarly, older adults often carry slightly higher weight than ideal-weight formulas suggest, which can actually be protective in some cases.

These calculators are commonly used by clinicians for quick screening, by dietitians when setting realistic weight goals, and by individuals tracking their fitness journey. When combined with BMI, body fat percentage, and waist circumference measurements, ideal weight gives a more complete picture of where you are and where you want to be.

How It's Calculated

This calculator uses five widely-cited formulas, all based on height and sex. Heights below 60 inches (152 cm) use linear extrapolation from the base value.

  • Hamwi (1964): Men: 48 kg + 2.7 kg per inch over 5 ft. Women: 45.5 kg + 2.2 kg per inch over 5 ft.
  • Devine (1974): Men: 50 kg + 2.3 kg per inch over 5 ft. Women: 45.5 kg + 2.3 kg per inch over 5 ft.
  • Robinson (1983): Men: 52 kg + 1.9 kg per inch over 5 ft. Women: 49 kg + 1.7 kg per inch over 5 ft.
  • Miller (1983): Men: 56.2 kg + 1.41 kg per inch over 5 ft. Women: 53.1 kg + 1.36 kg per inch over 5 ft.
  • BMI-based (18.5–24.9): Uses the healthy BMI range to back-calculate weight: Weight = BMI × Height(m)²

The displayed range is the minimum and maximum across all five formulas for the "healthy" band, giving you a practical window rather than a single number.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Don't chase a single number. The five formulas give a range on purpose — aim to be somewhere within it, not at one exact point.
  • Muscle weighs more than fat. If you strength-train regularly, your ideal weight may be at or above the top of the range and still be healthy.
  • Height accuracy matters. Even 2 cm difference in your reported height shifts the ideal weight by 1–2 kg. Measure barefoot in the morning for the most accurate result.
  • Use it alongside BMI and body fat. No single metric tells the full story. Combine this with our BMI Calculator and Body Fat Estimator for better insight.
  • Don't use ideal weight alone as a weight loss goal. Work with a dietitian or doctor to set safe, sustainable targets, especially if your current weight is far from the ideal range.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which ideal weight formula is most accurate?
No single formula is universally "best" — each was developed for different populations and clinical settings. The Devine formula is most commonly used in pharmacy for drug dosing. The BMI-based range (18.5–24.9) is the most widely recognized. Using the average across all five, as this tool does, gives the most balanced estimate for general health purposes.
Is ideal weight the same as goal weight?
Not necessarily. Ideal weight is a statistical estimate based on population data, while your personal goal weight should factor in your lifestyle, fitness level, age, and health history. Some people function best at the higher end of the range, others at the lower end. Discuss realistic targets with your healthcare provider.
Why are there different formulas for men and women?
Men and women have different body compositions — on average, men have more lean muscle mass and denser bones at the same height. The formulas account for this by using different base weights and per-inch increments, reflecting what healthy weight looks like for each biological sex at a given height.
What if I'm shorter than 5 feet (152 cm)?
Most classic formulas were designed for heights above 5 feet (152 cm). For heights below that, the formulas are extrapolated, which may make them less accurate. The BMI-based range remains valid at any height. If you're under 5 feet tall, the BMI healthy range (18.5–24.9) is generally the most reliable guideline to use.
