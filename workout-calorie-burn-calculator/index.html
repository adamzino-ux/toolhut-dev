Why This Matters

Knowing how many calories you actually burn during a workout is one of the most practical pieces of fitness data you can have. Whether you're trying to lose weight, maintain your current body composition, or fuel up properly before a long run, the numbers make a real difference.

Consider this: a 170-pound person burns roughly 270 calories in 30 minutes of moderate jogging — but only about 130 calories doing the same 30 minutes of light weight training. That's a 2× difference. Knowing this helps you plan smarter — maybe you pair a short HIIT session with a longer walk instead of spending an hour on the treadmill.

Calorie burn data also matters for post-workout nutrition. If you burn 600 calories on a long bike ride, replacing some of that energy (especially carbohydrates and protein) within 45 minutes improves recovery. Athletes, recreational gym-goers, and anyone tracking macros all benefit from accurate exercise calorie data. This calculator uses established MET (Metabolic Equivalent of Task) values from the Compendium of Physical Activities — the same methodology used by researchers and sports scientists.

How It's Calculated

This calculator uses the MET formula, the gold-standard method for estimating exercise calorie expenditure:

Calories = MET × Weight (kg) × Duration (hours)

Where MET (Metabolic Equivalent of Task) is a measure of how much energy an activity requires relative to sitting still. Walking slowly has a MET of ~3.5, while fast running hits MET 16+. Weight in kilograms is used because the standard formula was developed in metric units. Duration is converted to hours (so 30 minutes = 0.5 hours).

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Don't overestimate rest time. If your "45-minute workout" includes 10 minutes of rest between sets, calculate for ~35 minutes of actual activity.
  • Fitness level changes burn rate. As you get fitter, your body becomes more efficient — you burn slightly fewer calories for the same effort. That's normal progress.
  • Heart rate monitors are more accurate for individuals than MET formulas. Use this calculator for planning, and a chest strap or quality wearable for precise tracking.
  • HIIT burns more post-workout too. High-intensity intervals trigger EPOC (excess post-exercise oxygen consumption), meaning you continue burning extra calories for hours after. This calculator only shows in-workout burn.
  • Don't eat back all your calories unless you're an endurance athlete. Most fitness goals benefit from a modest calorie deficit even on workout days.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this calculator accurate for everyone?

The MET-based formula gives a solid estimate for average adults but can vary by ±10–20% depending on fitness level, body composition, metabolism, and workout intensity within the category. It's best used as a planning guide rather than a precise measurement. For more accuracy, use a heart rate monitor or metabolic testing.

Why does weight affect calorie burn so much?

Heavier bodies require more energy to move the same distance or perform the same movements — it's simple physics. A 220-pound person burns roughly 35–40% more calories than a 150-pound person doing the same activity for the same duration. This is why the formula multiplies MET by body weight.

Does age affect how many calories I burn?

Age is included in this calculator because metabolic rate tends to decline with age — roughly 1–2% per decade after age 30. Older adults also tend to have slightly lower muscle mass, which further reduces resting and active calorie burn. The effect is modest for short workouts but adds up over time.

How many calories should I burn per workout?

The American Heart Association recommends 150–300 minutes of moderate activity per week. A 200-calorie burn per 30-minute session, 5 days a week, puts you right in that sweet spot. Elite athletes may target 500–1,000+ calories per session, but for most people, consistency beats intensity.
