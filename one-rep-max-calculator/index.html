How to Use This One Rep Max Calculator

Enter the weight you lifted and how many reps you completed, then hit Calculate 1RM. The tool instantly shows your estimated one rep max across six different formulas, a training percentage breakdown, and a full rep-max table — no login, no signup, works offline.

For best accuracy, use a challenging set of 2–10 reps. Results become less reliable above 10–12 reps.

Why This Matters

Your one rep max is the foundation of intelligent strength programming. Nearly every major training system — 5/3/1, Conjugate, Texas Method, GZCLP — anchors its percentages to your 1RM. But testing your true 1RM is risky and taxing. A bad day, poor sleep, or minor injury can throw off the number. Submaximal testing is safer and often more reliable.

Practical example: a 185 lb lifter who squats 315 lbs for 5 reps gets an estimated 1RM of ~354 lbs (Epley). From that, they know their 80% training weight is ~283 lbs, their 70% is ~248 lbs, and so on. Instead of max testing every few weeks, they can track rep PRs and recalculate — a smarter, lower-injury approach favored by coaches like Jim Wendler and Mark Rippetoe.

Athletes use this to set competition attempts, coaches use it for team programming, and recreational lifters use it to gauge progress across months without the stress of true maximal attempts.

How It's Calculated

Six formulas are shown. All use w (weight lifted) and r (reps performed):

  • Epley (1985): w × (1 + r/30) — most widely cited
  • Brzycki (1993): w × 36 / (37 − r) — accurate for lower reps (1–10)
  • Lander (1985): w / (1.013 − 0.0267123 × r)
  • O'Conner (1989): w × (1 + 0.025 × r)
  • Wathan (1994): 100 × w / (48.8 + 53.8 × e^(−0.075 × r))
  • Lombardi (1989): w × r^0.10

The Epley formula is displayed as the headline result — it's the most commonly used and performs well across a broad rep range. The average of all six formulas is also a reasonable consensus estimate.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Stay in the 3–8 rep range for the most accurate estimates. The formulas assume reps to near-failure — grinding out 20 reps at 50% 1RM gives a poor prediction.
  • Don't use this to set attempt weights for a competition without also doing practice singles. Estimates can be 5–10% off under meet conditions.
  • The formulas assume a single straight set taken to or close to failure. Reps done in reserve (RIR) will underestimate your true max.
  • Re-test every 4–8 weeks as your strength improves — 1RM estimates go stale quickly during a productive training block.
  • Use the rep max table for accessory programming — e.g., if your 1RM bench is 250 lbs, your 8RM should be around 190–200 lbs, useful for hypertrophy programming.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate are 1RM calculators?

Within 5–10% for most people performing 3–8 reps to failure. Accuracy drops with higher rep sets (12+) and varies by individual muscle fiber type — fast-twitch dominant lifters tend to be overestimated by these formulas. Using the average of multiple formulas reduces individual error.

Which formula is most accurate?

Research is mixed, but the Brzycki and Epley formulas tend to perform best across most lifts and rep ranges. Brzycki is considered slightly better for low reps (≤10), while Epley handles a wider range. Running all six and taking the average is a conservative, reliable approach.

Can I use this for any lift?

Yes — bench press, squat, deadlift, overhead press, or any barbell movement. However, formulas tend to be less accurate for isolation exercises (curls, tricep extensions) since these involve smaller muscle groups where muscle endurance plays a larger role than in compound lifts.

What if I can only do 1 rep?

If you perform exactly 1 rep, all formulas return the weight you lifted as your 1RM — which is correct. That weight is your measured 1RM. The calculator is most useful when you want to estimate your max without actually performing a max attempt.
