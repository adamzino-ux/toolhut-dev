Why This Matters

Concrete patios are one of the most popular home improvement projects in the US — and for good reason. A well-poured 12×20 ft patio can add $5,000–$10,000 in home value while costing only $1,800–$4,500 to build. But surprise costs are common. Homeowners regularly underestimate by 20–30% because they forget about sub-base gravel, wire mesh, form lumber, concrete sealers, and finishing labor.

For example, a 240 sq ft patio sounds like a weekend project, but you'll need roughly 3 cubic yards of concrete (that's 3 ready-mix trucks or 54 bags of 80 lb mix), plus gravel, mesh, edge forms, and a float finishing tool. Stamped concrete can double the cost of a standard broom finish — sometimes jumping from $6/sq ft to $14/sq ft.

This calculator helps you budget confidently before calling contractors, so you can spot inflated quotes and make smarter decisions about DIY vs. hired labor.

How It's Calculated

The estimator uses these core formulas:

Square Footage = Length (ft) × Width (ft)
Cubic Yards = (Length × Width × Thickness_inches / 12) / 27
Total Cost = Material Cost + Labor Cost + Finish Upcharge

Concrete pricing: ~$130–$160/cubic yard for ready-mix (national average). Labor is calculated per square foot at $3.00–$5.50 for standard concrete work, then multiplied by regional and finish type modifiers. Stamped concrete, for instance, adds $6–$9/sq ft for labor and tooling. All figures include the waste percentage you set with the slider.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Always add a sub-base. 4" of compacted gravel under the slab prevents cracking and settling. Budget $1.50–$2.50/sq ft for this — many first-timers skip it and regret it.
  • Order 10% more concrete than calculated. Ready-mix trucks charge for partial yards, and running out mid-pour is a disaster. The waste slider handles this automatically.
  • Control joints every 8–10 feet. Cut or tool control joints before the concrete sets to prevent random cracking. This is free to do but often skipped by DIYers.
  • Cure properly. Keep fresh concrete moist for at least 7 days with plastic sheeting or curing compound (~$40–$80 for a 400 sq ft patio). Rushing curing leads to weak, dusty surfaces.
  • Get 3 contractor quotes. Concrete labor pricing varies widely — sometimes 50% between the cheapest and most expensive bid for the exact same job. Always compare.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a concrete patio cost per square foot?
A standard broom-finish concrete patio costs $6–$10 per square foot installed, including materials and labor. Stamped concrete runs $12–$18/sq ft, while a basic DIY pour can come in as low as $3–$5/sq ft if you do all the work yourself. Regional labor costs can significantly affect these numbers.
How many bags of concrete do I need?
An 80 lb bag of concrete mix yields approximately 0.60 cubic feet. Divide your total cubic footage (length × width × thickness in feet) by 0.60 to get the number of bags needed. For anything over 1 cubic yard, ready-mix delivery is faster, cheaper per yard, and gives better results than mixing bags.
Is a concrete patio a good DIY project?
Concrete work is physically demanding and time-sensitive — once it starts setting, you can't stop. A small patio under 150 sq ft is manageable for a motivated DIYer with a helper. Larger pours really benefit from professional labor, at minimum for the screeding and finishing. Form-building and prep work are great areas to DIY to save $500–$1,000 on a typical project.
Do I need a permit for a concrete patio?
Most municipalities don't require a permit for a simple ground-level patio under 200 sq ft that isn't attached to the house structure. However, rules vary significantly by city and county. Always check with your local building department before starting — unpermitted work can cause issues when selling your home.
